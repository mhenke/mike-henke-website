# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Tech recruiters, hiring managers, and technical decision-makers evaluating Mike Henke for senior engineering and leadership roles. They land on this site after seeing his name in a job application, LinkedIn profile, or conference talk. They spend 60-90 seconds forming a first impression; if it passes, they read deeper into the blog archive or career timeline.

## Product Purpose

Establish Mike as a credible senior technologist with 25+ years of experience who ships enterprise code, teaches what he knows, and is actively moving into security and AI. The site must communicate: I build things that work, I teach what I learn, I'm not a commodity developer.

## Positioning

Enterprise engineer who never stopped teaching. Same instinct from the ColdFusion conference circuit to the Union Pacific training room — just a different audience.

## Brand Personality

**Confident & Direct** — No-overselling, technical credibility over marketing speak. The design should feel like a senior engineer built it: clean, purposeful, not trying too hard. The voice is first-person, specific, and honest — no fabricated metrics, no taglines.

## Anti-references

- AI slop: generic gradient hero backgrounds, stock photo beach/desk setups, "Hello, I'm a Full Stack Developer" prose
- Cookie-cutter portfolio templates with timeline cards, icon grids, and "passionate about code" statements
- Dark-theme-with-neon-accent developer portfolios that look like every other Vercel/Next.js template (the cyan accent is intentional and restrained, not default)
- Unspecific testimonials ("Mike is amazing!") without concrete enterprise context
- Invented metrics or fabricated stats — every number on the site is real

## Design Principles

1. **Technical credibility through restraint** — Every element earns its place. No decorative clutter. The design demonstrates the same engineering values the content claims: clean architecture, no unnecessary complexity.
2. **Enterprise weight without corporate stiffness** — This is a $1.8M enterprise deliverable, not a side project. The site should feel like it came from someone who builds production systems, not someone who builds portfolio sites.
3. **Show the work, not the process** — Career timeline sections exist to contextualize achievements, not to demonstrate methodology theater. Concrete outcomes over abstract competencies.
4. **Dark theme as professional default** — The dark theme signals "I work in terminals and IDEs." It's the natural habitat of a senior engineer. Not edgy or countercultural — just honest.
5. **Voice continuity** — The same person who wrote ColdFusion conference talks and pushed Git adoption in 2009 is the same person running phishing training and tech deep dives in 2026. The site should tell one story, not two.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA minimum, target AAA where practical
- Reduced motion respected (already implemented in CSS)
- Keyboard navigation fully functional (skip links, focus states)
- Screen reader landmarks in place (already implemented)
- No reliance on color alone for information (already has proper contrast ratios)
